# Teacher Case Analysis - ISS Greenhouse Module

## Story problem

An ISS lettuce crop is alive but stunted. Roots curl into tangled balls instead of extending through the growth medium, and stems grow at irregular angles.

## Scientific phenomenon

Plants use multiple directional responses. Gravitropism normally provides a consistent orientation cue: roots generally grow with gravity, while shoots grow against it. In microgravity, gravity-sensing structures do not settle in one stable direction, so other cues become more important.

## Evidence map

| Source | Essential evidence | Instructional role |
|---|---|---|
| Crew | Roots do not consistently grow downward; repeated adjustments to nutrients, water, and lights did not solve the pattern | Establishes symptom and unsuccessful interventions |
| Sensors | Microgravity is the major environmental difference; nutrients, water, temperature, humidity, and lighting are nominal | Identifies the strongest causal difference and rejects resource explanations |
| Plants | Dense root tangles, poor anchoring, and odd stem angles | Provides direct biological observations |
| Logs | Defines gravitropism/statoliths and suggests alternative orientation cues | Supplies mechanism and design possibilities |

## Essential reasoning path

1. Root tangling is the observed symptom.
2. Normal nutrient, water, and light readings weaken common resource explanations.
3. The unusual environmental condition is microgravity.
4. Logs explain how gravity sensing normally supports directional growth.
5. Therefore, disrupted gravitropism best accounts for the evidence.
6. An engineering response should provide another consistent cue rather than simply adding more resources.

## Optional evidence

- Similar root anomalies occurred during prior missions.
- Plant pillows distribute water from multiple directions, reducing a moisture gradient.
- Directional lighting can help shoots but is less direct for roots.
- Physical root guides can supply a touch/structure cue.

## Distractors

### Nutrient burn

Tempting because leaves are pale and growth is stunted. Rejected because sensor readings are nominal and roots are tangled rather than showing a clear chemical-burn pattern.

### Incorrect light cycle

Tempting because stems are angled and leaves are pale. Rejected because the 16/8 schedule and light intensity are within expected range; the strongest abnormality is root orientation.

### Genetic seed defect

Tempting because all plants show similar symptoms. Rejected because the pattern is tied to the environment and appears across repeated plantings/missions.

### Water problem

Not a formal diagnosis, but a likely student idea. Water delivery is functioning. Uniform wicking may reduce directional moisture cues, but it does not explain the entire case as well as disrupted gravitropism.

## Strong classroom opportunity

The case introduces a reusable investigation routine:

- observation;
- evidence from different source types;
- elimination of alternatives;
- mechanism;
- solution criteria.

## Boundary notes

The game simplifies root behavior for a clear detective puzzle. Real plant responses in microgravity involve many interacting genetic, hormonal, mechanical, fluid, and environmental effects. Do not tell students that roots become completely random or that statoliths are the only gravity-sensing mechanism.
