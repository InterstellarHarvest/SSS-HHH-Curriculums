## Common technical issues

### The game does not load

- Refresh once.
- Confirm JavaScript is enabled.
- Try a current Chrome browser tab.
- Use the evidence fallback below if the problem is not solved within two minutes.

### Student cannot find a clue

- Remind the student to check all four source types.
- The Diagnose screen remains locked until formal clues are collected.
- Do not require every optional conversation branch.

### Student finishes very quickly

- Require completion of evidence reasoning, CER, and engineering response.
- Do not grade speed or game score.

## Evidence fallback

Provide this section when gameplay is unavailable.

### Mission briefing

A lettuce crop aboard the ISS is alive but stunted. Roots curl into dense tangles around the plant pillows rather than extending through the growth medium. Leaves are small and pale, and stems grow at unusual angles.

### Crew testimony

Astronaut Kim reports that the roots do not consistently grow downward. Adjusting nutrient levels, watering schedules, and LED positions did not solve the pattern. The same problem appears in every batch.

### Sensor record

- Environment: microgravity/apparent weightlessness
- Nutrients: nominal
- Water delivery: functional
- Temperature: 22 degrees C
- Humidity: 65%
- Light cycle: 16 hours on / 8 hours off
- Light intensity and spectrum: within expected range

### Plant inspection

Roots form dense, tangled mats and wrap around the plant pillow rather than anchoring deeply. Stems grow at odd angles but show some response toward the overhead lights.

### Mission log

Plants normally use gravitropism as a directional growth response. Gravity-sensing cells contain statoliths that settle in a consistent direction under gravity. In microgravity, the usual directional cue is greatly reduced. Suggested compensations include physical root channels, controlled moisture gradients, directional shoot lighting, or rotating growth systems.

Students use this evidence to complete the standard mission sheet.
