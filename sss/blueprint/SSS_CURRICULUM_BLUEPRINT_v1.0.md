# Space Sprout Sleuth Curriculum Blueprint

**Version:** 1.0  
**Game baseline:** `Space-Sprout-Sleuth` `main` at commit `2a6e8a7`  
**Curriculum repository:** `InterstellarHarvest/SSS-HHH-Curriculums`  
**Audience:** Middle school through adult/general public  
**Primary contexts:** Environmental science and STEM  
**Standards anchor:** NGSS  
**Status:** Approved curriculum architecture. This document governs the Curriculum Bible and prototype-lesson phases.

---

# 1. Purpose

This blueprint translates the completed Space Sprout Sleuth content audit into a repeatable classroom model.

It defines:

- what one 60-minute lesson looks like;
- what students do before, during, and after gameplay;
- what the teacher prepares and monitors;
- what documents exist for every case;
- how evidence collection, CER, vocabulary, and assessment work;
- how Campaign 1, Campaign 2, and the final SSS synthesis are organized;
- how the materials remain individually completable, accessible, printable, and suitable for unfamiliar teachers;
- how the project can later be adapted to a graduate-program lesson-plan template without redesigning the curriculum.

This is not the Curriculum Bible and not a set of finished lesson plans. It is the approved architecture from which those materials will be built.

# 2. Core instructional model

Each case is treated as a **one-period scientific investigation**, not as a game followed by a separate worksheet.

The student packet operates as an in-universe mission file that guides attention, captures evidence, and requires scientific reasoning while the student plays.

## 2.1 Non-negotiable lesson constraints

Every standard case lesson must:

- fit within one class period of 60 minutes or less;
- target approximately 20 minutes of gameplay;
- be individually completable;
- allow optional collaboration without requiring it;
- avoid homework and multi-day projects;
- allow students to consult the game dialogue and evidence;
- exclude gameplay skill and game score from academic grading;
- include a short Claim-Evidence-Reasoning response;
- end with an exit ticket;
- use chunked directions compatible with text-to-speech;
- print clearly in grayscale;
- distinguish established science, extrapolation, debated evidence, and fiction;
- include enough teacher support that an unfamiliar teacher can run the lesson.

## 2.2 Learning experience sequence

The standard case follows this arc:

1. **Launch the phenomenon**
2. **Set the investigation question**
3. **Play and collect evidence**
4. **Pause to process evidence**
5. **Diagnose and explain**
6. **Connect to the real world**
7. **Complete the exit ticket**

The worksheet should never ask students merely to copy every clue. It should require them to:

- sort evidence;
- identify patterns;
- reject alternatives;
- explain a mechanism;
- evaluate a solution;
- state uncertainty where appropriate.

# 3. Standard 60-minute lesson architecture

| Time | Phase | Student action | Teacher action |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 0–5 min | Entry and setup | Open game, packet, and case; read mission question | Confirm devices/headphones, display case code or directions |
| 5–10 min | Phenomenon launch | Make a brief prediction or identify what evidence is needed | Give concise framing; avoid revealing the diagnosis |
| 10–30 min | Gameplay investigation | Explore the case and record targeted evidence | Circulate, troubleshoot, prompt students to revisit sources |
| 30–38 min | Evidence checkpoint | Sort evidence, eliminate alternatives, identify mechanism | Use one short whole-class or individual check for understanding |
| 38–48 min | Diagnosis and CER | Submit game diagnosis; write concise CER | Support students who completed the game but need help reasoning |
| 48–55 min | Real-world connection | Complete a diagram, design decision, or short application | Clarify established science versus fictional case details |
| 55–60 min | Exit ticket | Answer one independent transfer question | Collect work and note misconceptions for future revision |

## 3.1 Flexible timing rules

- Gameplay may range from 15–25 minutes.
- Supplemental video or reading may replace part of the launch or application segment.
- A required video should remain under 10 minutes.
- A required reading should remain at one page or less.
- If device setup takes longer than expected, omit the optional real-world extension before cutting the CER or exit ticket.
- Students who finish early receive an optional extension on the same page rather than a separate required task.
- A student unable to complete the game may use a teacher-provided evidence summary and still complete the academic reasoning.

# 4. Student mission packet architecture

The default is **one double-sided sheet** per ordinary case. A second sheet is allowed only when the science genuinely requires a diagram, dataset, or one-page reading.

## Front side

### A. Student identification

The first page begins with a conventional classroom identification row at the absolute top of the printable area, above the Mission Title Block and all institutional or instructional content.

Default fields, in order:

- Name
- Date
- Period

The row appears only on the first page of a multi-page Student or Accessible worksheet. It is not repeated on continuation pages and does not appear on Teacher or Answer Key editions.

### B. Mission header

- Campaign and case number
- Case title and setting
- Mission question
- Three to five key vocabulary terms
- A short established-science / fictional-context marker when needed

### C. Initial thinking

One brief prompt, such as:

- What do you think is happening?
- What evidence would help distinguish the possible causes?
- Which system should be examined first?
- What variable might have changed?

This is not graded for correctness.

### D. Evidence task

The case-specific activity occupies the largest space.

Possible formats:

- evidence-source matrix;
- sequence/timeline;
- cause-and-effect chain;
- competing-hypotheses table;
- system model;
- spectrum or sensor comparison;
- structure/function diagram;
- design-constraint analysis;
- uncertainty ranking;
- policy evidence map.

Students should record only evidence needed for the reasoning task, not transcribe every game clue.

## Back side

### E. Diagnosis

- Selected diagnosis
- Biological or physical mechanism
- One rejected alternative and why it does not fit

### F. CER

A compact response:

- **Claim:** one or two sentences;
- **Evidence:** two or three specific pieces from different sources where possible;
- **Reasoning:** two to four sentences connecting the evidence to the mechanism.

### G. Real-world connection

One short application, comparison, or engineering decision.

### H. Exit ticket

One independent transfer question that can be answered in two to four minutes.

### I. Optional extension

A small box for early finishers. It must not affect the standard grade.

# 5. Teacher package for every case

Each case should contain the following source documents.

## 5.1 Formal lesson plan

Moderate detail, generally three to five pages:

- lesson title and overview;
- target audience and prerequisites;
- standards alignment;
- measurable learning objectives;
- success criteria;
- academic vocabulary;
- materials and technology;
- teacher preparation;
- estimated timing;
- launch/phenomenon;
- procedures;
- checks for understanding;
- differentiation and accessibility;
- likely misconceptions;
- assessment plan;
- real-world relevance;
- established science, extrapolation, and fiction notes;
- references;
- contingency plan for technical failure.

## 5.2 One-page quick-start guide

Designed for the classroom teacher or substitute:

- what students are doing;
- what to distribute;
- direct game link and case navigation;
- 60-minute timeline;
- correct diagnosis;
- essential science idea;
- likely sticking point;
- what to collect;
- technical fallback.

## 5.3 Teacher case analysis

One or two pages:

- story problem;
- scientific phenomenon;
- evidence channels;
- essential versus optional evidence;
- distractors;
- correct mechanism;
- why the wrong answers are tempting;
- instructional opportunities;
- serious misconceptions to prevent;
- fiction/extrapolation boundaries.

## 5.4 Answer key and annotated student packet

- acceptable evidence examples;
- model CER;
- alternate correct wording;
- common incomplete answers;
- scoring guidance;
- exit-ticket response.

## 5.5 Scoring tools

Every case includes:

- success criteria written in student-friendly language;
- quick rubric for ordinary classroom use;
- formal analytic rubric for portfolio or graduate-program use.

## 5.6 Technical and accessibility notes

- supported device assumptions;
- headphone use;
- game controls;
- text-to-speech guidance;
- troubleshooting;
- evidence-summary fallback;
- directions for students who cannot complete a gameplay challenge.

# 6. Assessment system

## 6.1 Academic grading principle

The academic grade measures:

- accurate evidence collection;
- scientific reasoning;
- vocabulary in context;
- explanation of mechanism;
- evaluation of alternatives;
- completion of the required written product.

The following are never graded academically:

- game score;
- speed;
- rank;
- rapport optimization;
- number of optional dialogue nodes visited;
- ability to discover bonus content.

## 6.2 Standard case quick rubric

Four dimensions, each judged on a three-level scale:

| Dimension | Secure | Developing | Beginning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Evidence | Uses specific, relevant evidence from appropriate sources | Uses some relevant evidence but misses an important source or detail | Uses vague, copied, or irrelevant evidence |
| Mechanism | Correctly explains how the cause produces the observed effect | Identifies the cause but explanation is incomplete | States a conclusion without a working mechanism |
| Reasoning | Clearly connects evidence to claim and addresses an alternative | Connection is partly explained | Evidence is listed without explanation |
| Communication | Uses key vocabulary accurately and completes required sections | Minor vocabulary or completion gaps | Major gaps make the reasoning difficult to follow |

No numeric point values need appear on the student page.

## 6.3 Formal analytic rubric

For graduate-program and research use, each dimension can be scored on a four-level scale:

1. Evidence selection
2. Evidence-source diversity
3. Causal/mechanistic reasoning
4. Alternative-explanation evaluation
5. Scientific vocabulary
6. Communication and completion

The formal rubric should remain behind the teacher materials unless needed for student self-assessment.

## 6.4 Pre-assessment

Before Campaign 1:

- one unfamiliar short plant-failure scenario;
- students separate observation from inference;
- students identify two needed evidence sources;
- students write a brief provisional explanation.

Purpose:

- establish baseline evidence-reasoning skill;
- identify misconceptions;
- support later comparison;
- avoid requiring prior space-botany knowledge.

## 6.5 Post-assessment

After both campaigns:

- two or three unseen mini-cases;
- at least one biological and one engineering/policy scenario;
- students select evidence, reject distractors, explain mechanisms, and acknowledge limitations.

The pre- and post-assessments should use parallel reasoning demands without repeating the same facts.

# 7. Accessibility and differentiation

## 7.1 Standard supports

Every student packet should use:

- short numbered directions;
- one task per visual section;
- plain-language directions followed by scientific vocabulary;
- sufficient white space;
- readable grayscale contrast;
- no meaning conveyed by color alone;
- labels compatible with screen readers and text-to-speech;
- consistent icons and section order.

## 7.2 Writing support

Because the standard version serves middle school through adults:

- use sentence stems only where they support reasoning;
- avoid turning every response into fill-in-the-blank;
- provide a CER frame that can be ignored by stronger writers;
- allow bullet-point evidence;
- require only one compact reasoning paragraph;
- define unfamiliar fictional terms beside the scientific term.

### Exact-match word banks for constrained blanks

Use a word bank when a fill-in-the-blank task requires a specific scientific, historical, or technical word or phrase that may be hard to generate even when the student understands the idea.

For each supported activity:

- include exactly one bank entry for each blank;
- include every expected answer and no unrelated terms;
- do not add decoys;
- repeat an entry when it is required more than once;
- keep multiword answers as complete phrases;
- place the bank beside the supported task on the same page;
- provide the same answer set in the Accessible edition;
- use the identical wording in the Answer Key.

This support is for constrained recall. It does not replace open-ended evidence, CER, explanation, design, or interpretation tasks.

## 7.3 Alternative access path

When gameplay is unavailable or inaccessible, the teacher can distribute:

- the case briefing;
- a one-page evidence digest;
- key screenshots or transcribed evidence;
- the same student reasoning task.

This preserves the learning objective without grading technical performance.

## 7.4 Extension without penalty

Advanced students may:

- evaluate a second solution;
- identify evidence limitations;
- redesign a habitat system;
- compare the fictional case with an Earth example;
- write a stronger counterargument.

Extensions are optional and do not raise the base requirement for other students.

# 8. Science, fiction, and uncertainty labels

Teacher materials use four categories:

## Established science

Supported mechanisms suitable for direct instruction.

Examples:

- gravitropism;
- physical pollination;
- spectral effects on photosynthesis;
- photoinhibition;
- ionizing-radiation damage;
- mycorrhizal mutualism.

## Plausible extrapolation

Real principles extended into a future engineering scenario.

Examples:

- rotating alien habitats;
- Europa greenhouse shielding designs;
- independent biological life-support controls.

## Debated or context-dependent evidence

Real research that should not be presented as universal or settled.

Examples:

- ecological effects of common mycorrhizal networks;
- metabolic effects reported in melanized fungi under radiation;
- outcomes of restoration inoculation.

## Fictional biology

Story mechanisms used to create a scientific reasoning problem.

Examples:

- alien VOC languages;
- karreth radiation-triggered metabolism;
- zhal-kelp pigment specificity;
- darkness-triggered alien signaling pathways.

Student pages should use brief markers only where misunderstanding is likely. Teacher pages should explain the boundary fully.

# 9. Case-by-case instructional identities

Every case uses a different primary thinking task so the curriculum does not become thirteen copies of the same clue log.

## Campaign 1 — Solar Agricultural Authority

| Case | Core science | Student identity | Primary task |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. ISS Greenhouse Module | Gravitropism in microgravity | Pattern investigator | Compare orientation evidence and design an alternative directional cue |
| 2. Lunar Greenhouse | Pollination without animal pollinators | Process modeler | Model the pollination sequence and identify the failed step |
| 3. Mars Habitat | Light spectrum versus total intensity | Data analyst | Compare spectral data and reject the “not enough total light” explanation |
| 4. Hayes Orbital Station | Reactor-specific photoinhibition | Timeline analyst | Correlate schedule change, reactor behavior, and control-system failure |
| 5. Europa Outpost | Radiation injury and shielding | Risk assessor | Weigh evidence, identify the damage pathway, and set shielding criteria |
| 6. First Contact Protocol | Chemical signaling and symbiosis | Systems modeler | Map atmosphere, signal, organism, and partnership interactions |
| 7. The Gift | Germination signal and missing environmental variable | Synthesis investigator | Combine prior evidence to identify a missing trigger and justify an intervention |

## Campaign 2 — Distant Soils: Federation Field Cases

| Case | Core science | Student identity | Primary task |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Vressk Centrifuge Habitat | Radial gravity gradient | Scale-and-systems engineer | Model how position changes apparent gravity and revise design criteria |
| 2. Ares Botanical Garden | Vibration-coupled pollination | Structure/function analyst | Match floral structure to the required pollen-release mechanism |
| 3. Oolian Mariculture Dome | Pigment-spectrum match | Energy-flow analyst | Evaluate usable light rather than total brightness |
| 4. Zhel’ii Diaspora Grove | Circadian timing and signaling | Pattern investigator | Build a schedule-to-signal timeline and distinguish correlation from mechanism |
| 5. Concord Botanical Vault | Species-specific requirements and universal policy | Assumption auditor | Identify where a universal safety rule fails under unusual evidence |
| 6. The First Garden | Mycorrhizal restoration and uncertainty | Restoration advisor | Develop a qualified systems model and recommend a monitored trial |

# 10. Campaign pacing

## 10.1 Campaign 1 recommended sequence

| Session | Lesson |
|---:|---|
| 1 | Pre-assessment and game orientation |
| 2 | Case 1 — ISS Greenhouse Module |
| 3 | Case 2 — Lunar Greenhouse |
| 4 | Case 3 — Mars Habitat |
| 5 | Case 4 — Hayes Orbital Station |
| 6 | Case 5 — Europa Outpost |
| 7 | Case 6 — First Contact Protocol |
| 8 | Case 7 — The Gift / Campaign 1 culmination |

Campaign 1 Case 7 should serve as both a playable story case and a culmination. Its student packet should require students to transfer the investigation routine developed across the earlier cases.

## 10.2 Campaign 2 recommended sequence

| Session | Lesson |
|---:|---|
| 1 | Campaign 2 orientation and short retrieval warm-up |
| 2 | Case 1 — Vressk Centrifuge Habitat |
| 3 | Case 2 — Ares Botanical Garden |
| 4 | Case 3 — Oolian Mariculture Dome |
| 5 | Case 4 — Zhel’ii Diaspora Grove |
| 6 | Case 5 — Concord Botanical Vault |
| 7 | Case 6 — The First Garden / Campaign 2 culmination |

Campaign 2 assumes experience with Campaign 1 but should still contain enough brief orientation for a returning class after a time gap.

## 10.3 Final SSS synthesis

One class period after both campaigns.

Students receive two or three short unfamiliar scenarios and must:

- determine what evidence is missing;
- identify the most defensible cause;
- explain a biological or physical mechanism;
- reject one distractor;
- recommend a constrained solution;
- identify one uncertainty or limitation.

This is not a large project or presentation. It is an individual mission certification.

# 11. Campaign culminations

## 11.1 Campaign 1 culmination — The Gift

The game case already acts as a narrative culmination.

The student packet should require:

- evidence synthesis across atmosphere, signaling, germination, and symbiosis;
- a systems diagram;
- a CER explaining the missing environmental trigger;
- one comparison to an earlier Campaign 1 case;
- an exit ticket asking students to identify what made this case harder than a single-variable failure.

## 11.2 Campaign 2 culmination — The First Garden

The final case should emphasize scientific uncertainty and policy.

The student packet should require:

- a qualified mycorrhizal systems model;
- evidence supporting missing compatible partners;
- evidence that does not prove guaranteed restoration success;
- evaluation of biosafety and restoration constraints;
- recommendation of a controlled, monitored trial;
- a CER that acknowledges uncertainty.

## 11.3 Final certification

Working title:

**Solar Agricultural Authority — Evidence Specialist Certification**

Format:

- one 60-minute individual assessment;
- compact mission dossier;
- no open-ended multi-day product;
- may use reference vocabulary but not answer keys;
- measures investigation practices more than memorized case facts.

# 12. External media and readings

External resources should be used only when they add information the game cannot efficiently supply.

## Required-media rule

A required video or reading must:

- directly support the case objective;
- fit within the 60-minute lesson;
- have a clear student task;
- avoid repeating the game dialogue;
- come from a strong source;
- include a simple citation line on student materials.

## Preferred source hierarchy

1. Universities
2. Museums
3. Scientific organizations
4. Peer-reviewed research
5. High-quality educational sources

Government sources may still be used when uniquely authoritative for NASA missions, spaceflight, or standards.

## YouTube constraint

Because student devices may not play YouTube:

- required YouTube content is teacher-projected;
- no individual student login or playback is assumed;
- worksheets must remain usable if the teacher omits an optional video.

# 14. Prototype lesson requirements

The first complete lesson should be:

**Campaign 1, Case 1 — ISS Greenhouse Module**

It is the best prototype because:

- the case is conceptually accessible;
- the phenomenon is visually understandable;
- the evidence structure is representative;
- the science is established;
- the lesson can demonstrate how fictional setting and real biology are separated;
- the worksheet can model evidence collection without becoming too dense;
- the case naturally introduces the investigation routine used by later lessons.

## Prototype deliverables

Student:

- one double-sided mission sheet;
- CER;
- exit ticket;
- optional extension.

Teacher:

- formal lesson plan;
- one-page quick-start;
- teacher case analysis;
- annotated answer key;
- quick rubric;
- formal rubric;
- references;
- technical fallback.

## Prototype evaluation questions

Before scaling, review:

- Can students understand the mission without excessive teacher explanation?
- Does gameplay remain near 20 minutes?
- Does the worksheet focus attention without spoiling the diagnosis?
- Is the writing load appropriate?
- Can students complete CER by minute 50?
- Does the exit ticket measure transfer rather than recall?
- Can an unfamiliar teacher run the lesson?
- Does the packet print clearly in grayscale?
- Does the lesson work when text-to-speech is used?
- Can a non-player complete the evidence-summary fallback?

# 15. Curriculum Blueprint decisions requiring future testing

These are working decisions, not permanent claims:

- one double-sided student sheet should be the default;
- 20 minutes is the target gameplay time;
- Case 7 and Campaign 2 Case 6 function as culminations;
- CER belongs in every case;
- the four-dimension quick rubric is sufficient for ordinary grading;
- the ISS case is the best prototype;
- one standard version can serve middle school through adults with optional supports.

Classroom testing may change any of these without invalidating the audit.
